# Hermeneian AI – Educational Concept Submission

Building AI course project  
Author: Dimitrios Christodoulou

## Summary

Hermeneian AI is a concept for an artificial intelligence assistant that supports professional literary translation, editing, and proofreading while preserving the author’s style, tone, rhythm, and narrative voice.

This repository is an educational concept submission for the Building AI course. It does not contain proprietary source code, private datasets, production prompts, client material, or internal system architecture.

## Background

My personal motivation comes from my work in publishing between 2020 and 2023. During that period, I saw first-hand how difficult it is to maintain high standards in book translation, editing, and proofreading. The main problems were not only speed, but also cost, accuracy, editorial consistency, and the uneven quality of human or machine-assisted translation workflows.

In many publishing projects, a translated book may pass through several hands: translator, editor, proofreader, formatter, and publisher. Without a consistent quality framework, the final result can suffer from changes in tone, inconsistent terminology, weak proofreading, loss of literary rhythm, and uneven editorial decisions across chapters.

Current generative AI tools can produce fluent text, but they do not automatically guarantee literary fidelity, chapter-level consistency, publisher-grade editing standards, or preservation of the author’s voice. This project is motivated by the need for an AI assistant that supports professional editorial judgment while keeping quality, consistency, and human responsibility at the centre of the process.

Common problems include:

* inconsistent tone between chapters
* loss of the author’s original voice
* weak proofreading after machine translation
* missed cultural or historical context
* expensive and slow editorial workflows
* difficulty comparing multiple translation or editing versions

This topic is important because books carry culture, memory, and identity. Poor translation can damage both the author and the reader’s experience.

## How is it used?

The system would be used by translators, editors, publishers, independent authors, and literary agencies.

A typical workflow could be:

1. The user uploads or pastes a chapter.
2. The system analyses the text for tone, style, vocabulary, rhythm, and consistency.
3. The user selects the intended task, such as translation, proofreading, stylistic review, or editorial comparison.
4. The system produces suggestions while explaining the reasoning behind major changes.
5. The human editor accepts, rejects, or modifies the suggestions.
6. The system keeps track of consistency across chapters or the full manuscript.

The system is intended to assist professional human judgment, not replace it.

## Data sources and AI methods

Possible data sources include:

* public domain literary texts
* user-provided manuscripts with permission
* translation memory created by the user
* dictionaries and terminology lists
* style guides
* editorial correction examples
* annotated before-and-after editing samples

Possible AI methods include:

* natural language processing
* semantic similarity analysis
* style and tone classification
* large language models
* retrieval-augmented generation
* consistency checking across long documents
* human-in-the-loop review
* quality scoring for translation and editing variants

The most important requirement is that the system should be transparent enough for a human editor to understand why a suggestion was made.

## Challenges

This project does not solve every problem in literary translation or publishing.

Important limitations include:

* AI may misunderstand irony, symbolism, or cultural nuance.
* Copyright and author permission must always be respected.
* Private manuscripts must be protected.
* Human editors remain responsible for final decisions.
* The system may perform differently across languages and literary styles.
* Evaluation of literary quality is partly subjective.

Ethically, the system should support authors, translators, and editors rather than reduce creative work to automatic text production.

## What next?

The next step would be to build a limited prototype focused on one clear task: comparing two versions of a translated paragraph and explaining which version better preserves tone, meaning, and literary style.

Future development could include:

* chapter-level consistency analysis
* translation quality reports
* editor dashboards
* terminology memory
* multi-language support
* publisher workflow integration
* manuscript-level proofreading
* explainable editorial suggestions

To move forward, the project would need collaboration with translators, editors, publishers, software developers, and copyright-aware data specialists.
